I've been writing code and making music for about the same amount of time.

For most of that time, I kept them in separate boxes. Engineering was how I made a living; music was the other thing. But the more I've made in both domains, the more I think that framing was wrong.

## The common thread

Both are acts of construction under constraint.

When you write a song, you're not free to do anything. You're working within a key, a tempo, a genre, a set of things you know how to do on the equipment you own. The creativity is in what you make *within* those constraints, not in transcending them.

Code is the same. You're not free to do anything. You're working within a language, a runtime, a team's conventions, a deadline, an existing system. The craft is in what you make within those constraints.

In both cases, the constraints aren't the enemy of creativity. They're the shape that creativity pushes against.

## What music taught me about engineering

Making music taught me to trust my ear before I trust my analysis. When something sounds off, it's usually off — even if the theory says it should work.

I've applied the same instinct to code. When something feels wrong in a design review — when the explanation feels too long, when the component feels too large — that feeling is usually tracking something real, even before I can articulate what.

The analytical mind is for articulating and testing what the intuitive mind already suspects. Not for overriding it.

## What engineering taught me about music

Engineering taught me that shipping a flawed thing is better than not shipping a perfect thing.

For years I sat on music that was "almost ready." The production wasn't quite right, or I wasn't happy with the mix, or the timing wasn't ideal. A shipping culture — where done is a value, and learning happens from real feedback rather than imagined feedback — broke that habit.

Make the thing. Put it out. Listen to what comes back. Iterate.

This applies equally well to lyrics and to APIs.

## Why this matters to me

I write about both here because both are genuinely part of how I think.

I'm not an engineer who moonlights as an artist, or an artist who happens to know how to code. I'm someone for whom both modes of making are necessary, and who finds that each one makes the other better.

This site is where those things live together.
