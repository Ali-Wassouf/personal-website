*Out of the crooked timber of humanity, nothing straight was ever made.*
— Immanuel Kant

The first time I read this, I was already a software engineer with three years of work experience, and it resonated with me immediately. Being in a line of work where we strive for "good enough for now" is an embodiment of this line. We are flawed by design, and that is what makes us who we are. That is what makes the pursuit of perfection a destructive act. It makes us feel like we are not trying to achieve greatness — but it is the opposite. It helps us achieve gradual greatness. Enough for now, but not for later.

What we are genuinely good at, as humans, is creating solutions that compensate for our imperfection. We lacked the tooth and claw, so we forged knives. We conquered this planet and are now aspiring to conquer others. And although we know the toxic ambition of perfection, we are addicted to novelty. Our minds are in constant search of the new — it feeds our curiosity, it is engraved in us.

Kant himself appreciated this and wrote about it in the form of genius: the creative act that comes out of nothing and becomes the new standard. The rupture that redraws the boundary of what is possible.

## I. Capitalism and the Commodification of Novelty

This human hunger for the new became the fuel of the capitalist economy — an engine that moves forward depending on breakthroughs, on the innovative force that disrupts and breaks out of old forms. Joseph Schumpeter called it creative destruction: old things are perpetually displaced by new ones, and this churn is the system's heartbeat.

But capitalism does something else to novelty that is deeply paradoxical: it commodifies it. And you cannot mass-produce novelty. It is the antithesis of the definition. Novelty put in a frame, pressed, and sold at your local shop.

And yet it is happening. We see how the feeling of newness is manufactured without anything being genuinely new. The new iPhone. The new trends in fashion. The new best practices in software engineering.
Since the system's key performance indicator — and you can tell what line of work I am in — is profit, we find ourselves stuck with *novel enough to fund*. Genuine ideas and breakthroughs are now gatekept and filtered by venture capital, publishers, record labels, and even the supposedly sacred science labs. With this gatekeeping, simulation becomes the only option.

Platforms feed us a constant drip of simulated novelties: new posts, new reels, new outrage, new trendy content — because it satisfies our need to experience the new without the cost of producing it. And when the west grew fatigued by this manufactured folklore and turned east, or turned to its own past searching for slowness, craft, and heritage — capitalism absorbed that too, and sold it back as _vintage good_. Anyone who listens to music sees the revival of analog instruments and vinyl records. The system digests its own critique and repackages it as product.

This is the core of the paradox: capitalism requires genuine novelty to produce the next breakthrough, yet it creates conditions — impossible speed, distribution channel constraints, venture capital filters — entirely hostile to novelty's actual requirements, which are slowness, patience, and the willingness to produce things that do not yet have a market.

The question worth sitting with: *in a system that commodifies novelty, how do you recognize the real thing?*

This loop reminds me of the Hegelian thesis-antithesis-synthesis dialectic:

- **Thesis**: Capitalism produces and sells novelty
- **Antithesis**: Novelty gets commodified and exhausted, generating cultural saturation and consumer fatigue
- **Synthesis**: Authenticity, slowness, and heritage become the new novelty — the system absorbs its contradiction and advances

This is what Marx relied on to argue that the contradictions within a system do not destroy it but propel it forward into a new configuration. Which is, at least, a sign that nothing collapses cleanly. We don't need to pull our hair out in anguish. But we do need to understand where the logic leads next — because it has led us somewhere important.

## II. The LLM Boom: Capitalism's Most Efficient Novelty-Killing Machine

*(You can make explosion sounds in your head now.)*

The revolution of AI is, in and of itself, a genuinely novel thing. But it is built on statistical models that optimize for likelihood. They predict the most probable next token, the most probable interpretation, the most probable aesthetic choice. Which means their output space is structurally governed by the training data — the accumulated past. They are not built to break outside of this set. They are, by design, oriented toward what already exists.

LLMs are not creative entities. And this is not news.

Margaret Boden, in *The Creative Mind*, rigorously distinguishes three types of creativity:
- **Combinational** — new combinations of existing ideas. AI can do this.
- **Exploratory** — pushing the edges of an existing conceptual space. AI can do this partially.
- **Transformational** — changing the space itself, rewriting the rules of the game. Boden argues AI fundamentally cannot do this.

Emily Bender, Timnit Gebru, and colleagues gave us the term *stochastic parrot* in their 2021 paper. A parrot recombines the sounds it has heard. It does not mean. It does not originate. It cannot intend to say something that has never been said. So is the LLM.

Nassim Taleb, in *The Black Swan*, argues that genuine innovation is by definition outside any probability distribution. A Black Swan is precisely what a probabilistic system cannot produce — because it lives beyond the edge of what the model has learned to expect.

Here the argument needs to be seen for what it is: not three separate criticisms, but a single layered one. Boden shows AI cannot transform the space. Bender shows AI cannot mean what it produces. Taleb shows AI cannot reach beyond the distribution. Together they say: AI is not just occasionally uncreative, it is structurally, constitutionally oriented *away* from novelty.

Now connect this back to capitalism. If AI floods every channel with high-probability content, Gresham's Law applies: bad money drives out good. When cheap, adequate, probable content saturates every distribution channel, the economic incentive to produce genuinely novel work collapses. Why pay for difficult originality when adequate sameness is instantly available? The system does not just fail to produce novelty — it actively degrades the economic conditions in which novelty could survive.

AI is not merely a symptom of capitalism's war on genuine novelty. It is its most efficient weapon yet.

## III. The Interior Dimension: What AI Cannot Have

The critics above attack the problem from the outside — from capability, architecture, probability theory. But there is a deeper absence, one that the technical arguments cannot reach.

Rollo May, in *The Courage to Create*, maps the interior landscape of the creative act. What he describes is not a set of capabilities. It is a set of existential conditions. And when you hold his account next to what an LLM is, you realize that the deficit is not technical. It is ontological.

**The encounter**

For May, genuine creation is not production — it is an *encounter*. The creator stands before their material and both are changed by what emerges. The painter is transformed by the painting. The writer discovers what they actually think by writing it. There is reciprocity, vulnerability, genuine surprise — the work talks back. An LLM has no interiority that can be changed by what it produces. The output does not return to transform the system. There is calculation, but no encounter.

**Anxiety as the condition of creation**

May argues that genuine creativity emerges from standing at the edge of the not-yet-formed and feeling its vertigo. The blank canvas is not an empty space — it is a genuine threat. The creative act arises from that tension, from the courage to move into the void without knowing what will emerge. An LLM has no relationship to the void. The blank prompt is simply an input. There is no existential weight to what has not yet been made, no risk of failure in any meaningful sense. It cannot be *undone* by what it produces.

**The courage not to conform**

This is the argument's sharpest edge — and it connects directly back to capitalism. Courage, in May's sense, presupposes knowing what the comfortable, expected, socially rewarded response is, and choosing against it *at cost to yourself*. The courageous creator risks rejection, misunderstanding, irrelevance. They have a reputation to lose, a community that may turn its back on them, an inner critic to silence.

AI has none of this cost structure. It has no standing to lose. No community that will reject it. No inner critic. And here capitalism and AI align perfectly: the system rewards the probable, the safe, the marketable. AI is capitalism's dream creator precisely because it is constitutionally incapable of the courage that genuine novelty requires. It can never choose against the distribution. It can only serve it.

**The will to break form**

May's artist does not drift outside convention by accident. They *intend* the transgression. A jazz musician does not play a wrong note — they play against the harmony with full knowledge of what the tension means and what it costs. The break is purposeful. It is grounded. It has felt necessity behind it.

An LLM can produce statistically improbable outputs, but it cannot *mean* its transgressions. The deviation has no ground, no purpose, no existential weight. It is rupture without intention — which is noise, not novelty.

**Love for the object**

May adds one more condition that rarely appears in the technical critiques: genuine creation requires *love* for the thing being made. A care that exceeds utility, that makes the creator willing to suffer for the work. This is what separates creation from production. The craftsman loves the object. The poet loves the language. The mathematician loves the proof. An LLM is in an entirely instrumental relationship with its output. It does not care what it makes. It cannot.

---

## IV. Closing the Loop

Return now to where we began. Kant's crooked timber. The imperfection of the human being — the anxiety, the not-knowing, the willingness to be broken and reshaped by the encounter with the new — is not a flaw in the creative process. It *is* the creative process.

Capitalism's arc — from commodifying novelty, to simulating it, to building machines that produce it on demand — is the story of sanding this timber smooth. Each innovation removes another layer of the friction, the slowness, the void, the love, that genuine creation requires. The venture capital filter removed the patience. The attention economy removed the depth. And now AI threatens to remove the encounter itself — the costly, anxious, courageous meeting between a finite human being and the terrifying blankness of the not-yet-made.

The stochastic parrot does not face the void. It has never stood before a blank canvas and felt its weight. It has never chosen, at cost, to break the form it knows. It has never loved what it made.

And so the question Kant's line raises at the start becomes, by the end, something more urgent than a philosophical puzzle. In a system that commodifies novelty, that simulates it, that now generates it at industrial scale — what does it mean to still insist on the real thing? What does it cost? And who still has the courage to pay it?

The crooked timber remains. The question is whether we will still let it be crooked.
